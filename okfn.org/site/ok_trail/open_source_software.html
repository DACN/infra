Open Source Software

What is Open Source Software?

The Open Source Software (OSS) movement grew out of the Free Software movement, and continues to work alongside it. The decision was taken to replace the term 'free' with 'open source' because of the ambiguity of 'free' (free as in free speech not as in free beer) and the anti-commercial connotations it held, but it ended up causing a mild philosophical schism. Free software advocates prefer to stress the freedom to modify and redistribute software, and operate a stricter license (so that all free software is open source, but not vice versa), while open source advocates say the essential element is unrestricted access to the source code.

These distinctions are insignificant when we compare the two with proprietary software, such as that peddled by Microsoft or Apple. The sourcecode for these programs is heavily protected, and so all development is done in-house. It is important to recognise, however, that both open and proprietary software may be distributed at a cost or for free, and that some open source software is distributed free for the same commercial reasons as proprietary software. It isn't simply a case of the hippies vs the corporations.

Why It Matters

What then are the advantages of OSS? Firstly, the open code means that anybody can modify the program to suit individual needs. Bugs are fixed much more quickly, because anyone competent can fix them, and the fix or 'patch' is then freely available on the internet. Applications are improved by a process of common consensus: if something is good, it is retained, if not, it is dispensed with. Much OSS is 'cross-platform', ie it works on any operating system. This reduces the monopolistic lock-in characteristic of proprietary software, whereby one's freedom to choose the best software is severely limited by what will run on a given system.

The ease of sharing code over the internet allows OSS to harness incredible power and generate collaboration on a global scale: many thousands of contributors worked on the development of the Red Hat Linux operating system, which, it has been estimated, embodies 8000 person-years of effort, or more than $1 billion in proprietary terms.

The Open Source model more closely reflects the underlying economics of software development. This millenium, over 90% of developers have been paid to come up with software that is specific to an organisation. This argues that the primary value of the software is to those who pay to have it developed, and there is therefore no compelling reason to protect the source code, or to restrict copying, which can be done at near-zero cost.

Furthermore, the low cost of OSS makes it ideal for use in developing nations. An example of this is the Simputer, a simple hand-held computer designed to bring mass computing to India. The Simputer Trust actively encourages OSS developers to port applications to its Linux operating system; the open code in turn allows local users to develop their own software. The boxes retail for $240.

The Threats from Patent Extensions

Many OSS developers fear the influence of software patents, in force in the US and currently being proposed in the EU. Seemingly low standards for 'inventiveness' have led to a shower of patent grants, the build up of 'patent portfolios' held by software companies, and even the appearance of firms specialising in patent litigation. While many of the major software firms have signed truce deals with each other, OSS coders have no resources to fight legal battles with extremely well-funded corporations, and a number of projects have had to be abandoned or never undertaken, such as OSS applications handling MPEGs, JPEGs and Dolby files. The development of open Internet telephony software is severely hampered by a patent thicket allowing telephone companies to stifle innovation and promulgate their monopolies.

The Position of the Open Knowledge Foundation

The Open Knowledge Foundation encourages the development and uptake of OSS, and actively supports the campaign of the Foundation for a Free Information Infrastructure against the introduction of software patents in Europe.
