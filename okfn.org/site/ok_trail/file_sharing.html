The Open Knowledge Trail - File Sharing Peter Lockley 2005-01-23 2005-04-21

File Sharing

The Current Situation - The System's Not Working

Currently it is illegal to download music and films for free over the internet. Nevertheless, many many people do so: an estimated 20 million in the US alone. A law that criminalises such a large section of the population calls for serious reconsideration.

Freely available artistic content is socially desirable, in fact, the only reason to prohibit the practice at all is that artists are currently compensated for their work by revenue derived from sales of music, usually in CD format, and the rise of file-sharing is perceived to be jeopardising this.

Attempts to discourage the practice are:

  • Ineffectual: exhortations to 'say no to piracy'.
  • Draconian: the prosecutions carried out by the Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA) on children and students.
  • Technologically debilitating: the use of Digital Rights Management (DRM) that greatly decreases interoperability of systems, prohibits fair and creative re-use of material and may infringe human rights.
  • Invasive of privacy: ISPs are required to divulge the identities of their clients, and the content industry is pushing for an obligation to disclose details and terminate contracts with alleged infingers - in other words you could be guilty until proven innocent.

Solutions

The Open Knowledge Foundation, following the suggestions of other academics and organisations, proposes a system whereby:

  • all works of film or music for which an artist desires compensation are issued with a digital code;
  • these works are registered with an expanded version of the Copyright Office, for a fee that would cover administrative costs, and then made freely available over the internet;
  • the Copyright Office collect usage data; on the basis of which
  • copyright owners are remunerated in proportion to popularity of their work.

The money to reward artists would come from an increase in income tax, or, less efficiently but more plausibly, from a tax on the services used to access content, chiefly ISPs (Internet Service Providers).

Estimates for the US market give the required tax in the first year as 12%. Even if the figure for the UK turned out to be twice that, your monthly ISP charge might rise from 20 to 25 pounds, for all the free, legal, high-quality, ad-free films and music in the world.

Would you pay?

Further Reading

  • 'Promises to keep: Technology, Law and the Future of Entertainment' by William W. Fisher III. In this book the author, a Professor at the Harvard Birkman centre, proposes a mechanism of free internet distribution of music and film with rewards based on usage, funded by income or ISP tax.
  • 'Rewards versus Intellectual Property Rights' by Shavell and Ypersele. Formal economic analysis of the two systems, concluding that an optional reward system is unambiguously optimal. Introduction comprehensible to non-specialist, but still quite hard.
